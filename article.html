The new geography of defence procurement: why the Middle East is reshaping global supply chains

Paul — Director of Global Inquiries | 19 Mar 2026 | Defence Trading, Dubai UAE
Share in 𝕏 ✉ ⎙

As NATO spending accelerates and Middle Eastern nations invest heavily in domestic defence capability, the procurement landscape is shifting in ways that are creating new opportunities — and new complexities — for licensed traders and prime contractors alike.

The convergence of three major forces — European rearmament, Gulf domestic investment, and African security demand — is creating a procurement environment unlike anything we have seen in the post-Cold War era. Licensed intermediaries with established trade routes, regulatory compliance frameworks, and vetted supplier networks are more relevant than ever.

"The Middle East is no longer simply a buyer. It is becoming a hub — a transit point, a manufacturing base, and a regulatory gateway for compliant international defence commerce."

European rearmament is driving unprecedented demand

SIPRI data confirms that global defence spending reached a record $2.2 trillion in 2024 — a third consecutive year of real-terms growth. NATO member states in Central and Eastern Europe have increased defence budgets by an average of 8.3% year-on-year, driven primarily by the ongoing conflict in Ukraine and new commitments to the 2% GDP target.

The practical consequence is a sustained shortfall in ammunition, artillery systems, and protective equipment across European theatres. Traditional Western manufacturers — many of whom operated at peacetime production rates for over a decade — cannot meet demand at pace. This gap is being filled, compliantly and legally, through licensed intermediaries operating from jurisdictions with the regulatory infrastructure to facilitate rapid cross-border procurement.

The UAE as the world's defence trade hub

The UAE's position is unique. It holds active defence trade licences that permit procurement from both Eastern and Western supply chains. It maintains credible end-user certification frameworks. And it hosts the world's premier defence exhibition — IDEX — which in 2025 generated over $5.2 billion in signed contracts.

Over $80 billion in defence contracts signed at IDEX since 2019 have passed through UAE-based intermediaries. The country's neutrality, regulatory maturity, and logistics infrastructure make it the natural hub for procurement flows that might otherwise be blocked by political constraints in other jurisdictions.

What this means for procurement officers

For procurement officers operating within NATO structures or allied governments, the opportunity is to work with UAE-licensed traders to access non-NATO standard munitions (122mm, 152mm artillery rounds, 82mm mortars, 7.62×54R), protective equipment categories, and manufactured-to-spec components that Western production pipelines cannot deliver at required timelines.

  • Non-standard ammunition replenishment remains the single highest-demand category across European and Middle Eastern buyers
  • Protective equipment — particularly NIJ-certified ballistic protection — is seeing a second wave of demand as reserves drawn down during conflict replenishment programmes are reassessed
  • Communications gear and C4ISR integration components are increasingly being procured through UAE channels as supply chain diversification becomes a strategic priority

Looking ahead

The geopolitical environment of 2026 is likely to sustain elevated defence procurement activity across all three regions — Middle East, Europe, and increasingly Africa. The Sahel, Horn of Africa, and Arabian Peninsula continue to see active counter-insurgency operations driving sustained demand for mortar systems, light weapons, and tactical equipment.
