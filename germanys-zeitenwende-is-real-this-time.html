Germany's Zeitenwende is real this time — and the numbers finally back it up

Editorial Team — Defence Trading |26 Feb 2026 |Europe
Share

Olaf Scholz's Zeitenwende speech of 27 February 2022 was greeted, in some quarters, with a degree of strategic scepticism that experience had earned. Germany had, over the previous three decades, managed to acknowledge virtually every major security challenge in Europe without meaningfully adjusting its defence posture. The pattern was well established: political recognition of a problem, a public commitment to address it, and then a procurement process of such baroque complexity that the commitment quietly expired before it translated into capability. The history of German defence spending from the end of the Cold War to the Russian invasion of Ukraine is largely a history of promises made and budgets delayed.

The current cycle is different. Germany's defence budget reached €73.4 billion in 2024, representing 2.08% of GDP — the first time Germany had hit the NATO 2% target in the alliance's modern era, and the crossing of a threshold that Berlin had been avoiding for years with creative accounting and definitional debates about what counted as "defence" spending. The €100 billion Sondervermögen — the special off-balance-sheet defence fund announced alongside the Zeitenwende speech — has been largely committed to specific programmes, removing the most obvious mechanism by which ambitious defence announcements typically disappear into bureaucratic process.

The industrial response

Rheinmetall's order book tells the story more succinctly than any policy document. It has tripled since 2021 — from approximately €15 billion to over €50 billion across its defence segment. The company is building new facilities, hiring at scale, and operating at production rates that its peacetime staffing model was not designed to sustain. The KF51 Panther tank programme, the IRIS-T air defence system expansion that has become one of the most in-demand platforms in Europe, and the commitment to F-35 procurement are all moving — not at the pace of an emergency, but at the pace of a government that has made genuine institutional decisions rather than political gestures.

"For a country that spent decades making peace with its military past, Germany is now writing cheques that its grandfathers would have found astonishing."

The real bottlenecks are not financial but industrial and human. Skilled labour in the German defence sector is genuinely scarce. The Bundeswehr itself has been recruiting hard and still falls short of authorised strength in several specialties. Defence-specific manufacturing — ordnance, vehicle armour, propellants — requires workforce competencies that take years to build and that Germany systematically allowed to atrophy during the post-Cold War period. The companies have the orders and the capital; what they lack, in meaningful quantities, is the trained human capacity to fulfil them at the required pace. This constraint adds 18 to 24 months to virtually every contract timeline, and it is not something that political will can resolve quickly.

The cultural dimension

What makes the German case genuinely interesting — and genuinely complicated — is that the industrial transformation is happening simultaneously with a cultural one. Germany is not simply a country that is spending more on defence; it is a country that is actively renegotiating its relationship with military power in ways that carry significant social weight. The debates about conscription revival, about German troops on the eastern flank, about arms exports to conflict zones, are not peripheral to the Zeitenwende — they are central to whether it becomes durable policy or reverts to the historical pattern of impressive announcements followed by quiet retreat.

The political evidence, at present, suggests durability. Across the main party spectrum, there is a degree of convergence on the necessity of sustained defence investment that would have been unthinkable in 2021. The AfD's complicated relationship with NATO and Russia creates its own pressures, but has not so far derailed the fundamental consensus. The generational shift in German public opinion toward accepting military power as a legitimate instrument of national policy is real, measurable in polling data, and consequential for the long-term trajectory of European defence architecture.

Germany's reindustrialisation of its defence sector is the single most significant development in European security since the Cold War ended — not because of what Germany is buying, but because of what a militarily serious Germany means for the entire European defence industrial and political structure. The anchor of EU defence integration has, for the first time in a generation, the political will and the financial commitment to play that role. The capability will follow. It is taking longer than the speeches suggested, but it is coming.
