The defence industry just had its best decade since the Cold War — and the next ten years look even better for suppliers

Editorial Team — Defence Trading |14 Feb 2026 |Global
Share

Global defence spending crossed $2.443 trillion in 2025 — a number that would have seemed implausible as recently as 2019, when the world was still operating on the assumption that the post-Cold War peace dividend had been spent but the underlying security environment remained broadly stable. For the third consecutive year, every major region increased its military spending in real terms. Europe's growth trajectory is the steepest it has been since the early 1980s. The Middle East has not seen a single country reduce its defence budget since 2020. The Indo-Pacific is in a procurement cycle that shows no sign of plateauing. The structural drivers of this spending — Russia-Ukraine, China-Taiwan tensions, Middle East instability, Sahel deterioration, North Korean provocations — are not going away in any foreseeable timeframe.

For prime contractors, the numbers are extraordinary. Lockheed Martin's order backlog stood at $176 billion at its most recent quarterly report — a figure that represents approximately three years of revenue at current delivery rates. RTX reported an order backlog of $206 billion. BAE Systems' operating profit was up 14% year-on-year, driven by sustained demand across its land, air, and maritime segments. These are not one-year windfalls produced by a specific conflict; they are the financial expression of a sustained multi-year procurement cycle that has fundamentally changed the risk-return profile of the defence industry compared to the decade before.

The supply chain squeeze

The most consequential dynamic in the current defence market — and the one that is least visible in the prime contractor headline numbers — is the squeeze on Tier 2 and Tier 3 suppliers. The primes are ordering. The supply chain beneath them, in many critical categories, cannot keep up. Component shortages across precision guidance electronics, titanium forgings, propellant precursors, and certain specialty steels are creating bottlenecks that add months to delivery timelines and significant costs to programme budgets. The primes' backlogs are, in a meaningful sense, partly a function of their inability to convert orders into deliveries at the contracted pace, and the constraint is upstream in the supply chain.

Precision guidance electronics present the most acute shortage. The same semiconductor scarcity that affected automotive and consumer electronics manufacturing has hit defence procurement hard, and the defence sector's relatively small volumes mean it has less market power to resolve the constraint than larger commercial buyers. Titanium forgings — required for aircraft frames, naval vessels, and certain ground vehicle components — have been affected by the combination of demand surge and supply chain disruption from Russia, which was a significant titanium supplier before 2022. Propellant availability remains tight, with global production running substantially below the demand implied by current munitions orders.

"Defence is no longer the sector that polite society avoided at dinner parties. The pension funds noticed. The sovereign wealth funds noticed. The question now is whether supply can keep up with demand."

The opportunity for alternative supply chains

The supply chain constraints at the prime and Tier 1 level create the structural opportunity that licensed intermediaries and alternative supply chain operators are positioned to fill. When Western prime contractors cannot deliver 155mm ammunition on contracted timelines, buyers look elsewhere. When titanium forgings are on 18-month lead times from traditional Western suppliers, procurement officers explore alternative sourcing with appropriate compliance frameworks. The UAE's position as a licensed procurement hub — able to access both Western and non-Western supply chains within a compliant regulatory framework — is precisely adapted to this environment.

Defence is now a growth industry in the way technology was in the 2010s — attracting capital, talent, and entrepreneurial energy that it had not seen since the Cold War ended. The ESG complication has not disappeared: some institutional investors remain reluctant to hold defence equities, and certain funds maintain explicit exclusion policies. But the trend is unmistakably toward normalisation. Sovereign wealth funds, many of them from regions where defence spending is a first-order political priority, have been increasing defence allocations consistently. European pension funds, confronted with the strategic environment their beneficiaries inhabit, have been quietly revising the assumptions that led them to divest defence exposure in the 2010s. The moral clarity that once made defence an uncomfortable investment category has, for better or worse, been complicated by a security environment that makes the alternative — inadequately funded armed forces — look rather less comfortable.

The question for the next decade is not whether demand will remain elevated — the structural drivers are durable — but whether the industrial base can expand quickly enough to meet it. The answer, based on current production trajectories, is probably not at the pace that procurement timelines require. That gap — between what governments need and what Western industry can deliver at contracted timelines — is the defining commercial opportunity in the global defence market for the foreseeable future.
